It’s also rough on workers in the industry who receive low pay and don’t often remain in jobs.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">The situation has gotten so bad that business groups from across Pennsylvania <a href=\"https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5c2e545d0dbda3cf1389658c/t/6674721e07349c7dc880c6f1/1718907422801/Sign+on+Letter+-+Updated+6-20-24.pdf\">recently implored</a> lawmakers to intervene, arguing that “virtually all economic sectors” are affected by the shortage. Working parents can’t find child care so employers can’t fill open positions.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Pennsylvania lawmakers’ solution to this problem has typically been tax credits, which lower the amount of money a resident owes the state’s Department of Revenue. </p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Last December, lawmakers agreed to expand Pennsylvania’s child and dependent tax credit. As a result, families got a bump in the amount they could claim for child care costs on their 2023 tax returns. </p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">And the recent state budget <a href=\"https://www.pahouse.com/InTheNews/NewsRelease/?id=134835\">includes</a> a tax credit for Pennsylvania businesses that contribute to their employees’ child care expenses.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">However, local chambers of commerce say that tax credits fail to address the structural issues that plague the child care industry — namely, a serious provider shortage driven by low wages. Casey Foundation, a youth advocacy charity.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><strong>How else does Pennsylvania help families afford child care?</strong></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">The Child Care Works program subsidizes child care for low-income families, and is funded by the state and federal governments.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Currently, 96,809 children receive subsidized child care through this program, according to the Pennsylvania Department of Human Services. Advocates tell How We Care that providers often are unable to accept new children due to a lack of staff. </p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><strong>How bad is Pennsylvania’s child care provider shortage?</strong></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Last summer, the statewide coalition Start Strong PA — which advocates for accessible, affordable child care — commissioned a survey of 762 child care programs. For example, the state limits the number of 1- to 2-year-olds who can be supervised as a group to no more than 12, with a minimum of three staff members. </p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><strong>What do advocates want the state to do?</strong></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Advocates for the child care industry say more government subsidies and grants are needed so that centers can raise employee wages.</p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">Start Strong PA <a href=\"https://www.pennaeyc.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/RecruitmentandRetentionReport-R1-1.pdf\">cites</a> programs in Illinois, Nebraska, and Tennessee that provide salary supplements to early childhood workers. </p><p style=\"text-align: left;\"><br/></p><p style=\"text-align: left;\">The coalition of chambers that wrote the letter to state legislators is working with Start Strong PA to expand child care access.
