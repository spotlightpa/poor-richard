Doug Mastriano (R., Franklin) over how to conduct a forensic audit of last year&#39;s election in Pennsylvania has been making headlines nationally since it burst into public view last week.<br/>\n<br/>\nBut underlying that story is a lesser-known one about far-reaching powers that come with being in one of the top legislative leadership posts in Pennsylvania. <br/>\n<br/>\nAt the start of every session, legislators in the Democratic and Republican caucuses in both chambers gather behind closed doors to elect a slate of leaders. In doing so, rank-and-file lawmakers hand them the reins to <a href=\"https://www.spotlightpa.org/series/the-hidden-tab/\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">the millions of dollars that the legislature gets to spend every year</a> — and all the perks that flow from there. <br/>\n<br/>\nIf a lawmaker wants to hire someone to staff their Capitol office, they have to get the blessing of their caucus leaders. Corman also removed Mastriano from his chairmanship of the legislative committee that was pursuing the forensic audit. Corman gave few details about his decision, saying only that Mastriano was &#34;<a href=\"https://www.inquirer.com/politics/pennsylvania/jake-corman-doug-mastriano-pennsylvania-republican-election-audit-20210820.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">more concerned with grandstanding,</a>&#34; and had failed to get the job done.<br/>\n<br/>\nThough Mastriano and his supporters cried foul — <a href=\"https://www.facebook.com/SenatorDougMastriano/posts/1186501975094937\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">countering that it was Corman who had tried to obstruct the audit work at every turn</a> — he ultimately had little recourse. <br/>\n<br/>\nIt was a power move that hasn&#39;t been seen in more than 15 years. According to interviews with current and former legislators and staffers, those types of actions were far more common in the 1980s and 1990s — and were almost always perceived as punitive. <br/>\n<br/>\nOne of the last times it happened was in 2005, during a politically volatile time in the Capitol.
