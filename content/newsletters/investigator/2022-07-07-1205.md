Here’s what you need to know about the case and what a favorable ruling would mean for the state. <em>—<a href=\"mailto:ecoston@spotlightpa.org\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">Ethan Edward Coston</a>, Spotlight PA</em><br/>\n<br/>\n<strong><span style=\"font-size:20px\">What is the theory? </span></strong><br/>\n<br/>\nThe “independent state legislature doctrine” is a legal theory that <a href=\"https://www.spotlightpa.org/news/2022/03/pennsylvania-congressional-map-lawsuit-independent-state-legislature-doctrine/\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">claims state courts do not have oversight power over election policy set by state legislatures</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\nPeople who subscribe to this theory assert that courts should not be allowed to intervene in how legislatures draw political lines — even in instances where they are unfairly drawn for partisan benefit, a practice known as gerrymandering — or weigh in regarding the constitutionality of election laws.<br/>\n<br/>\n<span style=\"font-size:20px\"><strong>Which case will the court consider?</strong></span><br/>\n<br/>\nThe U.S. Supreme Court agreed to take <a href=\"https://www.supremecourt.gov/search.aspx?filename=/docket/docketfiles/html/public/21-1271.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #009edb;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">a case from North Carolina</a> during its next session, which begins in October.<br/>\n<br/>\nIn early 2022, a group of voters sued to have the state’s new congressional and legislative maps thrown out for unfairly favoring Republicans.
