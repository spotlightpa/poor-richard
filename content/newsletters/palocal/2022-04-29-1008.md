exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">inextricably link the two cities</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\nBy the turn of the 21st century, 10% of Erie&#39;s population was Black and nearly half its Black residents had family ties to Laurel, the paper adds.<br/>\n<br/>\nDixon, now in her 80s and living in nearby Meadville, is still an educator and serves as director of the Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. <a href=\"https://campussuite-storage.s3.amazonaws.com/prod/1559001/66603da0-49ad-11ea-a7a6-0ae97245cb8b/2066257/72b7eb26-6522-11ea-b531-0aaa28114a49/file/MLKInfo.pdf\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">Mentoring Program</a> at Meadville Area Middle School. <br/>\n<br/>\nWith the 68th anniversary of the <em>Brown v. Board of Education</em> ruling weeks away, we talked to Dixon about her experience desegregating Stainton, the “beautiful” moments in a hellish year, reflections on her career, and what she found in the North. </p>\n\n<p dir=\"ltr\" style=\"margin: 10px 0;padding: 0;mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: left;\"><em>Our conversation has been edited for clarity and length. </em><br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local:</strong> How were you chosen to come to Stainton? <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> The school’s superintendent received a telegram [from authorities] saying by morning the large city schools must be integrated. I was already teaching at an all-Black school in town.  <br/>\n<a href=\"https://www.meadvilletribune.com/news/dixon-reflects-on-life-of-educational-leadership/article_2d0aa530-6cd5-11eb-86a7-2790ac289b02.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\"><img align=\"right\" data-file-id=\"1954860\" height=\"236\" src=\"https://mcusercontent.com/77370ff1d001f9bb991fed9e7/images/7e018e68-9f05-e004-aab9-1193219a2013.jpeg\" style=\"border: 0px initial;width: 200px;height: 236px;margin: 15px 0px 15px 15px;outline: none;text-decoration: none;-ms-interpolation-mode: bicubic;\" width=\"200\"/></a><br/>\nSo I got a call in the middle of the night. It was something else in my house that night, honestly. We didn’t go back to sleep. We talked about what had happened to other people who had done this, including those <a href=\"https://abcnews.go.com/US/wireStory/orleans-marks-anniversary-girls-integrating-schools-81163718\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">children in New Orleans</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\nI had to think about it. But my parents, who were deep into civil rights, reminded me that they and so many others had been fighting for a long time for that day to come. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local:</strong> So you took the job as the school’s part-time librarian. How were you treated?<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> The principal said that no students were to use the library while I was there. And it wasn’t much of a library to begin with, you know. I really needed to establish one.<br/>\n<br/>\nBut the most beautiful thing about being there, and perhaps the only beautiful thing, was that each day when the students lined up for their classrooms after recess, when they passed by the library and their teacher couldn’t see them, they would wave at me and smile. I always looked forward to that. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local:</strong> Would all or most of those children have been white?<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> There was one Black student whose parents allowed him to come in as the first Black kid at that school. I’ve tried but to this day I can’t remember his name.<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local: </strong>You told the Erie Times-News that you took the job in Erie because your father was ill and you needed to earn more money. What were your impressions of the city?<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> Things weren’t too different [from the South] at the time. It’s just that they tended to be more subtle. Blacks also had hard times getting homes. There was a riot my first week there <a href=\"https://www.sharedheritage.org/index.php/timeline/\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">in 1967</a> — it wasn’t at East High School where I taught, but my goodness.<br/>\n<br/>\nI also formed a lot of friendships that were lasting. The staff at East High School made such a difference in my life. They looked after me. One of my friends helped me get a house because I couldn’t get a house. And that allowed me to bring my parents to Erie. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local: </strong>There are still very few teachers of color there or <a href=\"https://www.wesa.fm/education/2020-09-11/half-of-pa-schools-still-dont-have-a-single-teacher-of-color-black-educators-explain-why\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">statewide</a>. Why do you think that is?<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> I’ve seen a lot of improvements in Erie public schools. ... But we have to keep working to tear down the walls [when it comes to opportunities for teachers of color everywhere]. It seems like a never-ending battle. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local:</strong> Do you have any plans to retire? <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> No, I’m just going to take my orders from the good Lord. When he tells me, I’ll retire. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>PA Local:</strong> Did you ever go back to Stainton Elementary? <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Dixon:</strong> Oh yes. And when I got there I was greeted by the principal again. Only this time it was an African American woman.<strong> </strong><br/>\n<br/>\n—<em><a
