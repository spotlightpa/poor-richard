Black military airmen.<br/>\n<br/>\nAlford was a Mississippi native and skilled technician who attended the Tuskegee Institute (now Tuskegee University) in Alabama, where the airmen trained. She majored in home economics and worked her way through college by fixing the squadron’s primary training aircraft. <br/>\n<br/>\nAlford would move to New Brighton with her husband, a native of Beaver County, after college. She went on to become the first Black home economics teacher at New Brighton High School in the 1950s. After receiving her master’s degree from Michigan State, she was hired as the first Black guidance counselor at Beaver Falls High School in the 1960s. <br/>\n<br/>\nRegis Bobonis Sr. — a historian, researcher, and Pittsburgh’s first Black TV news reporter — spoke with the Beaver County Times in 2015, one year before his death, recalling the first time he learned of Alford in a casual conversation with a relative.<br/>\n<br/>\n“The family member was where I was doing research and he said, &#39;You know there’s a woman,&#39;” Bobonis told the paper.<br/>\n<br/>\nAlford died in 2011, her remarkable life <a href=\"https://www.timesonline.com/story/news/2011/01/23/rosa-mae-willis-alford/18361933007/\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">recounted in this obituary</a>. Two years later, the airmen memorial bearing her name was dedicated in Sewickley. She is the only woman listed. <br/>\n<br/>\nNearly 1 in 10 Tuskegee Airmen <a href=\"https://www.heinzhistorycenter.org/blog/western-pennsylvania-history-the-tuskegee-airmen/\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">called Western Pennsylvania home</a>. Under pressure in 1940, several years before the outbreak of World War II, President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s War Department established a flight program at historically Black colleges. They included the Tuskegee Institute, where a civilian pilot training program had launched years prior under the direction of a Bryn Mawr native named Charles Alfred Anderson, “<a href=\"https://billypenn.com/2023/06/24/black-aviators-philadelphia-forsythe-anderson-tuskegee/\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">the father of Black aviation</a>.”<br/>\n<br/>\n“There was already this consciousness on the part of African Americans in relation to the military and the role African Americans played in the 20th-century U.S. Army,” Black said. “The door was already open for African Americans to have different roles.”<br/>\n<br/>\nAlthough white pilots were not allowed to fly more than 52 missions, the Tuskegee Airmen often flew up to 100 missions due to a lack of replacements, according to a Pitt-funded documentary: &#34;<a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EpR6wFz1MEE\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">Fly Boys: Western Pennsylvania&#39;s Tuskegee Airmen</a>.&#34;<br/>\n<br/>\nBy the end of World War II, the Tuskegee Airmen had racked up thousands of combat stories, missions, medals, and commendations. But that didn’t guarantee acceptance. <br/>\n<br/>\nElaine Effort’s father, the late Sgt. Vernol Leapheart, was crew chief for then-Col. Benjamin O. Davis Jr., commander of the Tuskegee Airmen during World War II.<br/>\n<br/>\n“They had a lot of racist comments about Blacks and their abilities,” Effort, a retired Pittsburgh news reporter, told PA Local. “They said, ‘We don&#39;t believe Blacks will stay and fight.’ The Tuskegee Airmen trained and trained, and they weren’t using them in action.”<br/>\n<br/>\nEffort continued: “Finally Eleanor Roosevelt came down to Tuskegee. She made a point by getting in a plane. Word came from the White House that someone told them not to let her do it. She went up anyway. The assistant later said, ‘Have you ever tried to tell Elenor Roosevelt what to do?’”<br/>\n<br/>\nIn 2010, Bobonis discovered several men from Pittsburgh had been Tuskegee Airmen. He began to organize a memorial soon after.<br/>\n<br/>\n“The more they looked into it the more they thought, ‘Oh wow, we really should do something to honor these guys,’” said Effort, who was part of the planning. “It seemed like it came together quickly. Once we started working on this, the pieces started falling into place.”<br/>\n<br/>\nIt is the largest outdoor Tuskegee Airmen memorial in the country. Another memorial is displayed in Concourse A of the Pittsburgh International Airport. <br/>\n<br/>\nSome Tuskegee Airmen are still alive, <a href=\"https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rxZ4TxkdeZQ\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">carrying on the legacy</a>. <br/>\n<br/>\n“It is such a brilliant story.
