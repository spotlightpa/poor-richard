Without the state pushing tons of fish into waters each year, opportunities to catch them would be considerably narrower and demand for fishing licenses and permits — a primary, almost exclusive source of funding for the state&#39;s <a href=\"https://www.fishandboat.com/Fish/Stocking/StateFishHatcheries/Pages/default.aspx\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">14-hatchery network</a> — would likely wane. <br/>\n<br/>\nThis came to a head in 2018 with state lawmakers <a href=\"https://www.inquirer.com/philly/news/pennsylvania/fish-boat-commission-funding-fishing-trout-harrisburg-20180306.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">threatening to oust</a> then-director of the Fish and Boat Commission John Arway over a proposed fishing license fee hike that he said was needed to sustain the state&#39;s hatchery program. Arway retired months later. <br/>\n<br/>\nThe financial tension has since dissipated some: a reserve fund is being <a href=\"https://www.post-gazette.com/life/outdoors/2020/05/28/Pennsylvania-fish-boat-commission-strategic-reserve-funding/stories/202005280110\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">spent down</a> and the pandemic saw a sizable increase in fishing license applications. Boating revenue is also up, Parker added.  <br/>\n<br/>\nThere is other tension, though, including <a href=\"https://lancasteronline.com/sports/outdoors/trout-war-group-wants-stocking-of-hatchery-trout-over-wild-brook-trout-to-end/article_9b7f760e-197c-11ec-8b38-17e276b70bfe.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">a warning</a> that hatchery-raised trout threaten to overwhelm the already stressed wild brook trout, Pennsylvania’s only native species. (Supporters of stocking hand-raised fish say doing so can help protect the natives from overfishing.)<br/>\n<br/>\nParker said the commission is aware of the concern and studying the issue, adding, “as an agency we’re dedicated to the conservation of wild trout, but part of our mission is also to provide recreational fishing opportunities.”  <br/>\n<br/>\nBeyond the obvious differences, aqua-farmed trout, like those raised in hatcheries, are considered less wily and evasive than their wild cousins. (A retired fisheries biologist named Ray White compared it to “turning barnyard chickens <a href=\"https://www.anglersjournal.com/freshwater/taking-stock-the-contentious-world-of-hatcheries\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">loose in the forest</a>.”) <br/>\n<br/>\nBut your average recreational fisher is unlikely to care, and without Pennsylvania’s stocked trout, they’re less likely to land one at all.
