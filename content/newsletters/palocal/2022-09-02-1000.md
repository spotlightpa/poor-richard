100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;word-break: break-word;color: #3b3b3b;font-family: &#39;Open Sans&#39;, &#39;Helvetica Neue&#39;, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;font-size: 16px;line-height: 125%;text-align: left;\">\n                        \n                            <p dir=\"ltr\" style=\"margin: 10px 0;padding: 0;mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #3b3b3b;font-family: &#39;Open Sans&#39;, &#39;Helvetica Neue&#39;, Helvetica, Arial, sans-serif;font-size: 16px;line-height: 125%;text-align: left;\">“Wild-simulated” ginseng is grown in a wild setting — privately owned forest lands, for example — but planted by humans with seeds, the same found in the berries atop the plant’s leafy crown, though many are purchased from vendors online. <br/>\n<br/>\n“We’re encouraging people to have their own patch,” botanist Chris Firestone of Pennsylvania&#39;s Department of Conservation and Natural Resources <a href=\"https://www.nationalgeographic.com/animals/article/ginseng-root-appalachia\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">told National Geographic</a>. “Because then they’re not out taking it.”<br/>\n<br/>\nWhile some experts believe forest farming or “conservation through cultivation” is the ancient herb’s best long-term hope, there was almost no way of knowing how commonplace the practice was until a Penn State botanist named Eric Burkhart launched a years-long confidential study of Pennsylvania’s ginseng sellers to find out.<br/>\n<br/>\n“We learned that somewhere approaching three-in-10 people at this point are saying that when they sell the ginseng they&#39;re not necessarily reporting it as wild-simulated for a variety of reasons, but nevertheless it’s something that they planted,” Burkhart said. “So there&#39;s no doubt that this practice is catching on. How long it&#39;s been going on is unclear.”<br/>\n<br/>\nBurkhart said the real number could be as high as five-in-ten. <br/>\n<br/>\nThe reluctance to openly identify wild-simulated ginseng as such stems, in part, from the fact that buyers — almost all of them in China and South Korea — place a premium on roots grown without human interference. (Completely wild or “spontaneous” ginseng is the most sought-after form, but wild-simulated ginseng can pass, as long as no one knows.) <br/>\n<br/>\nSeveral harvesters declined on-the-record interviews with PA Local.<br/>\n<br/>\nGinseng farming can’t happen just anywhere either. The herb is best suited for the shady slopes of forest lands, and in Pennsylvania, where most forest land is privately owned, that means private property. (No ginseng harvesting is allowed on public lands here.) <br/>\n<br/>\nPrivate landowners might plant a patch themselves, or strangers might use their land to do so without their permission or knowledge. “Property ownership boundaries are not necessarily the most important thing to a guerilla ginseng planter,” Burkhart explained. <br/>\n<br/>\nPoachers remain a common concern, especially in light of news stories <a href=\"https://www.wthr.com/article/news/local/police-woman-held-after-ginseng-hunter-was-fatally-shot-near-salem/531-0fb2f52e-9406-47ce-84dc-7a46a1595126\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">like</a> <a href=\"https://www.google.com/url?q=https://www.daytondailynews.com/news/man-sentenced-ginseng-slaying-case/6UYmbgS43kTvkU3eLmBCZP/&amp;sa=D&amp;source=docs&amp;ust=1662086706573910&amp;usg=AOvVaw3Z4wZsbPJ-zzEB9oQU3mx7\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">this</a>. But some scientists are also worried that forest farming won&#39;t be widespread or fast enough to offset the impacts of non-sustainable (and often untracked) wild harvests in a truly meaningful way. <br/>\n<br/>\nIf it is, there&#39;s the added hurdle of improperly sourced seeds from outside Appalachia that could <a href=\"https://www.pennlive.com/life/2021/08/ginseng-farming-in-pennsylvania-may-threaten-native-plants-gene-pool-study.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">muddy the plant&#39;s gene pool here</a> and make the species less resilient, and the benefits less genuine over time. <br/>\n<br/>\n“We’re hoping to come up with a plan for preserving what genetic stock is left out there that is truly local or regionally adapted material, and figure out how we get that into nursery production or seed banking or some kind of cryopreservation state,” Burkhart said.  “It’s a slow and tedious process and from my standpoint it’s not going quickly enough.”<br/>\n<br/>\nBurkhart knows that as long as ginseng remains valuable, the harvests won’t stop. (Of eight monitored wild ginseng plots on DCNR land, he said six were wiped out by poachers in recent years.)<br/>\n<br/>\nHe also knows that forest farming could dominate ginseng production in the not-so-distant future, adding: “We’re going to need that planting stock to get that industry going and make sure it is well-adapted. We need to propagate and protect what’s left.”<br/>\n<br/>\nPennsylvania has a dealer licensing program for ginseng. Of the 19 U.S. states that actively export the plant, Pennsylvania&#39;s above-board production ranks somewhere in the middle. <br/>\n<br/>\nBut last year’s total harvest here was a record low at roughly 600 pounds, a fraction of the 3,500-pound high reached in 1990, the year after record-keeping began.<br/>\n<br/>\nBurkhart isn’t sure what to make of it. He wonders if it’s yet another sign of the plant’s continued decline, or a sign that segments of the historically secretive industry are going back underground.<br/>\n<br/>\n—<em><a
