what you will about Breezewood, but you’d be hard-pressed to find a more culturally relevant bathroom break anywhere in the world.<br/>\n<br/>\nBest known to scores of motorists as the awkward go-between for two of the nation’s largest highways, Breezewood has attained internet infamy thanks to the 14-year-old and <a href=\"https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/breezewood-pennsylvania/photos/sort/oldest\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">heavily memed</a> photo above. <br/>\n<br/>\nThe image by Edward Burtynsky, whose “quest to photograph a changing planet” was <a href=\"https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2016/12/19/edward-burtynskys-epic-landscapes\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">chronicled by the New Yorker</a> in 2016, appeared in <a href=\"https://www.edwardburtynsky.com/projects/books/oil\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">his book on the fossil fuel industry</a> and was far more difficult to produce than the finished product suggests.<br/>\n<br/>\nBurtynsky told Bloomberg in 2019 that it required days of scouting, a scissor lift, and a compression-heavy telephoto zoom: “<a href=\"https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-07-24/why-the-internet-loves-this-pennsylvania-rest-stop\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">I kept trying and trying, and no, no, no</a>.”<br/>\n<br/>\nIn an email sent to PA Local this week, Burtynsky’s office said it’s “fully aware how much this photograph has become ingrained into the cultural zeitgeist.”<br/>\n<br/>\nOne reprint sold for $43,750 at Sotheby&#39;s — and <a href=\"https://twitter.com/mikesbarnes/status/1560340646375956483?s=20&amp;t=xXJrgwzy868W-3vCStNV7g\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">they continue to circulate</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\nThere is an entire genre of memes — dating back <a href=\"https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/breezewood-pennsylvania\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">at least a decade</a> — built on the visual, one that shows a quarter-mile strip of claustrophobia-inducing asphalt that has, in a popular sense, come to symbolize everything from the excesses of car culture and the corporatocracy, to the “unbearable sameness” of everything.<br/>\n<br/>\n(Think pieces keep going there, too — in <a href=\"https://www.nationalreview.com/2021/05/in-defense-of-breezewood/\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">both</a> <a href=\"https://beltmag.com/breezewood-pennsylvania-most-american-place-earth/\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">directions</a>.)<br/>\n<br/>\nAs a result, Breezewood is recognizable to legions of people who have never actually been to the &#34;traveler&#39;s oasis&#34; or set foot on its logo-covered shores.<br/>\n <br/>\nWhile the &#34;neon jumble&#34; seen in Burtynsky&#39;s photo functions as the antithesis of emotional attachment for the millions of motorists forced to pass through it every year, others can picture the place differently.<br/>\n<br/>\nPA Local reader Don Hebel lived in Breezewood in his younger years, in a house that&#39;s no longer standing. He explained:<br/>\n<br/>\n“I have an entirely different perception of the place and its morph from a small town of good and kind neighbors, three mom-and-pop stores, a post office, barber shop, two churches, a very good grade school with great teachers, a few gas and service stations, several family owned motels with family farms, and fields surrounding all but the budding commercial strip.&#34;<br/>\n<br/>\nHebel left in 1965, when the strip <a href=\"http://www.gribblenation.org/2015/11/aerial-photos-of-breezewood-1958-and.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">looked comparatively quaint</a> and the transition from local owners — like those chronicled by the <a href=\"https://www.bedfordgazette.com/news/breezewood-town-of-motels/article_946b2731-c718-5ec9-8f2b-97515d3618d2.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">Bedford Gazette here</a> — was beginning to intensify. (It was around this time that the stretch of I-70 connecting Breezewood to the Maryland state line and points south was completed. National Geographic says that&#39;s when &#34;<a href=\"https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/breezewood-the-neon-jumble\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">things got weird</a>.&#34;) <br/>\n<br/>\nIn a 2004 piece on the closure of The Post House cafeteria and bus station after <a href=\"https://www.post-gazette.com/uncategorized/2004/06/27/Breezewood-s-Post-House-is-closing-its-doors/stories/200406270150\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">41 years in business</a>, the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette took note of the local shift &#34;from a handful of motels and family restaurants to a constellation of chain hotels — Holiday Inn Express, Comfort Inn, Econo Lodge — and almost any fast food a person could desire.”<br/>\n<br/>\nIn a 2020 piece for Gribblenation titled “The rise and decline of a highway rest stop,” Adam Prince says that trend <a href=\"http://www.gribblenation.org/2018/02/will-we-ever-see-breezewood-bypass.html\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">cuts the other way, too</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\n“Over the last ten years, a number of fast food chains have left,” Prince wrote of Breezewood. “Wendy&#39;s, Burger King, KFC, and Taco Bell have all closed” as the travel plazas have expanded, built food courts, and consolidated services. (There is still plenty of fast food to be found.)<br/>\n<br/>\nIf Breezewood’s monument to commerce is slowly crumbling and/or consuming itself, there is one thing certain to finish it off: the unlikely creation of a demanded-but-long-thwarted highway bypass.<br/>\n<br/>\nA push for one by Michael Dawida, a former Democratic state senator from Pittsburgh who was involved in a rear-end collision in Breezewood in 1988, led instead to a <a href=\"https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB932939160483361786\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">widening of the roadway</a>.<br/>\n<br/>\nToday, a number of the businesses seen in Burtynsky’s iconic 2008 photo are gone. In another 14 years the frame is almost certain to look even more different. But the internet never forgets.<br/>\n<br/>\n—<em><a href=\"mailto:cdeppen@spotlightpa.org?subject=PA%20Local%20feedback\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule:
