<strong>The Centre County Board of Commissioners on Tuesday <a href=\"https://www.centredaily.com/news/local/article276207401.html\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">approved</a> the controversial Responsible Contractor Ordinance by a vote of 2-1,</strong> with Democrats Mark Higgins and Amber Concepcion voting in favor. Republican Steve Dershem voted against it.<br/>\n<br/>\nThe ordinance is slated to go into effect July 27 and will only apply to Centre County Government’s public construction contracts that total more than $250,000, according to reporting from Halie Kines, the Centre Daily Times’ local government reporter.<br/>\n<br/>\nWe spoke with Kines about her ongoing coverage of the <a href=\"https://www.centredaily.com/news/politics-government/article276492741.html\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">ordinance debate</a>. Our conversation has been edited for length and clarity.<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>What is the Responsible Contractor Ordinance? How will it change current practice? </strong><br/>\n<br/>\nWhat the RCO really does is define what “responsible” means for Centre County public projects. The county code requires the Board of Commissioners to award public works contracts to the lowest responsible and responsive bidder. But because there is limited guidance on what “responsible” means, the county proposed a Responsible Contractor Ordinance with those guidelines and requirements. <br/>\n<br/>\nA big talking point of the RCO is that it will require the county to use contractors with at least 70% of the craft labor workforce employed on the project (to) be enrolled in or (have) completed an apprenticeship training program, or a journeyperson. But there are many other things listed in the ordinance that play into the safety factor. For example, a contractor can’t bid on a project if they’ve been convicted of any crime relating to its contracting business in the past 10 years.<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Why do supporters believe the ordinance is necessary?</strong><br/>\n<br/>\nWorker safety is a top goal of this ordinance. As many pointed out during the public hearing Tuesday, five construction workers have <a href=\"https://www.centredaily.com/news/politics-government/article274780756.html\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">died on the job in Centre County</a> in the past five years. So I think that alone shows it can happen anywhere, including Happy Valley. The RCO requires the contractors to have satisfactory records of past performances and a crew with [Occupational Safety and Health Administration] safety training, among other things. <br/>\n<br/>\nSupporters also say it’ll promote workforce development and help protect the taxpayers in Centre County, as RCOs lead to fewer change orders, delays and cost overrun on jobs and ensure high-quality work is done.<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>What are the potential negative ramifications?</strong><br/>\n<br/>\nOpponents to the RCO fear it will limit who is able to bid on county projects and will prevent smaller, local contractors from (doing) county work. An example given goes like this:<em> I have a small construction company of 50 employees, and most have been working here for decades. They have 20 (or) 30-plus years of experience in the field, but because they didn’t go through an apprenticeship training, they won’t qualify to work on these projects. Why is one form of training better than another? </em><br/>\n<br/>\nWith that, some worry that the ordinance is too restrictive and local contractors won’t be able to meet the requirements, leading to more people from outside of Centre County working on local projects. Others are concerned that it will increase the costs of future county projects and will <a href=\"https://www.centredaily.com/news/politics-government/article275016996.html\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">favor unions</a> over nonunion companies. <br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>What are the biggest misconceptions about the ordinance?</strong><br/>\n<br/>\nThe ordinance only applies to public construction contracts with the Centre County Government over $250,000. It doesn’t force other municipalities to follow the ordinance, and it doesn’t say anything about unions.<br/>\n<br/>\n<strong>Is there anything else readers should know about the RCO? </strong><br/>\n<br/>\nTime will tell how this works out for Centre County. Other counties, municipalities, and school districts in Pennsylvania have adopted similar RCOs, so it’s not a new concept. In the past, the county hasn’t had many projects that would fall under this ordinance, but it will have at least one large project — the <a href=\"https://www.centredaily.com/news/local/community/bellefonte/article260351670.html\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">renovation</a> of the former Centre Crest building — in the near future that will fall under the ordinance. <br/>\n<br/>\nI really encourage residents to read the entire <a href=\"https://centrecountypa.gov/DocumentCenter/View/25256/Ordinance-1-of-2023-Responsible-Contractor-Ordinance\" target=\"_blank\" style=\"mso-line-height-rule: exactly;-ms-text-size-adjust: 100%;-webkit-text-size-adjust: 100%;color: #5d9eca;font-weight: bold;text-decoration: underline;\">ordinance</a> on the county’s website.
